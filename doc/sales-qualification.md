# Sales Conversation & Qualification Logic

## Goal
Decide fit, not persuade.

## 0) Mindset (non‑negotiable)
Your job is not to convince. Your job is to decide.

If you feel pressure during the call, it means:
- the lead isn’t qualified, or
- you’re trying to justify yourself.

Both are signals to slow down or exit.

---

## 1) Pre‑call qualification (real gate)
### 1.1 Hard filters (must be explicit in the form)
The form must clearly state:
- Minimum engagement starts at $5,000
- CEO only
- Data access required
- This is not tool implementation or coaching

If they still apply, they’ve self‑selected.

### 1.2 What you look for before accepting the call
Accept the call only if:
- Company revenue ≥ $1M
- CEO is the applicant
- They describe structural problems (not tools)
- They don’t ask about discounts or tactics

If any of these fail → no call.
Default answer is no, not yes.

---

## 2) Call structure (30–45 minutes, no more)
The call has 4 phases. If you skip one, you lose control.

### Phase 1 — Frame the conversation (5 minutes)
Opening script (memorize):

“This conversation is not a sales pitch. My goal is to understand whether your situation fits our methodology. If it does, I’ll explain what working together looks like. If it doesn’t, I’ll tell you quickly.”

If they react badly to this → bad fit.

### Phase 2 — Diagnose the real problem (15–20 minutes)
You are not gathering requirements. You are testing structural pain.

Core diagnostic questions (do NOT ask all, pick 6–8):
- “How do you currently understand what’s happening across the business?”
- “If I asked you for a clear operational snapshot right now, how would you get it?”
- “Where do teams disagree most on numbers or priorities?”
- “How many tools does the business rely on daily—and who owns the truth?”
- “What breaks first when volume increases?”
- “What did growth make harder instead of easier?”
- “What decisions take longer than they should?”
- “Where do you feel you’re compensating manually?”

What you are listening for:
- Discomfort
- Frustration
- Awareness of fragmentation
- Inability to clearly explain the system

Not listening for:
- Intelligence
- Ambition
- Enthusiasm

If they describe problems cleanly but don’t feel pain, they’re not ready.

### Phase 3 — Introduce the Core Source Model (10 minutes)
Frame it short and controlled:

“What you’re describing is exactly what happens when growth breaks repeatability. We don’t fix this with tools or tactics. We fix it by restoring a single operational source of truth.”

Then briefly outline:
- The 4 phases
- Tools come after structure
- Methodology, not consulting‑on‑demand

Do not explain how. Do not show solutions yet.
If they push for details → they’re shopping.

### Phase 4 — Fit decision & next step (5–10 minutes)
If it’s a fit:

“Based on what you’ve shared, this looks like a Core Alignment case. The next step would be a structured diagnostic phase. Engagements start at $5,000 and scale based on complexity.”

Then stop talking. Let silence do the work.

#### Objection handling (no defense)
- “That’s expensive.” → “It usually is—for businesses that aren’t ready.”
- “Can we start smaller?” → “Smaller usually means incomplete. Incomplete alignment makes things worse.”
- “We just need help with X.” → “That’s a symptom. We don’t treat symptoms in isolation.”
- “We need to think about it.” → “Of course. This only works if you’re ready to address it properly.”

No follow‑up chasing.

---

## 3) Decision outcomes (only 3 allowed)
Choose one:

✅ **Proceed**
- Outline next steps
- Send a simple engagement summary
- No proposal theater

❌ **Disqualify**
- Say it directly and respectfully
- Do not offer alternatives

⏸ **Pause** (rare)
- Only if timing is the sole issue
- No nurturing sequence

---

## 4) Internal red flags (instant disqualifiers)
End the process immediately if:
- They negotiate price
- They won’t share data
- They want tool recommendations first
- They want you to “work with the team” without authority
- They talk more than they listen

These are not sales challenges. They are structural misfits.

---

## 5) What good sales feels like
When this works:
- You are calm
- You talk less than the CEO
- You are okay losing the deal
- You feel relief when you disqualify someone

If sales feels stressful, you are violating your own rules.
